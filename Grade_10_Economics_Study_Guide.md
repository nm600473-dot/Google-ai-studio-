# Unit 1: Theory of Consumer Behaviour

## 1.1 The Concept of Utility
### 1.1.1 Definition and Nature of Utility
* **Core Definition**: In economics, **Utility** is the level of satisfaction or pleasure derived by a consumer from consuming or possessing a good or service. It represents the "want-satisfying power" of a commodity.
  * *Example*: Bread has the utility to satisfy hunger; a cup of water has the utility to quench thirst.
* **Aesthetic/Pedagogical Principle**: It is vital to emphasize that utility is not an objective physical property of the good itself, but a subjective, psychological relationship between the consumer and the commodity.

### 1.1.2 The Relativity of Utility
Utility is characterized by its high subjectivity and relativity. It is **never absolute** and depends on several dimensions:
1. **Relativity to Person (Subjectivity)**: Different people derive different levels of utility from the exact same item.
   * *Example*: A non-smoker derives zero (or even negative) utility from cigarettes, whereas a smoker derives high utility.
2. **Relativity to Time**: The utility of the same commodity varies across different times of the day, seasons, or years.
   * *Example*: A thick winter jacket provides immense utility during the cold rainy season (Kiremt) but zero or negative utility during the hot dry season (Bega).
3. **Relativity to Place**: The utility of a good changes based on geographical location.
   * *Example*: An umbrella provides higher utility in rainy regions compared to desert lowlands.

### 1.1.3 Utility vs. Usefulness
A classic misconception among Grade 10 students is treating "utility" and "usefulness" as synonymous.
* **Usefulness**: An objective state of being functional, beneficial, or helpful for physical well-being. It is product-centric.
* **Utility**: A subjective state of satisfaction, regardless of whether the product is healthy or socially desirable. It is consumer-centric.
  * *Example*: Cigarettes or alcoholic beverages possess high **utility** for an addict because they satisfy a strong desire, but they possess little to no **usefulness** (and are indeed harmful to health).

### 1.1.4 Approaches to Measuring Utility
Since utility is a qualitative, internal mental state, measuring it poses a historical challenge. Economists address this through two main theoretical frameworks:
1. **Cardinal Utility Approach**: Postulates that utility is quantitatively measurable in absolute numerical terms using subjective units called **"utils"** or monetary values.
2. **Ordinal Utility Approach**: Postulates that utility cannot be measured in absolute numbers; instead, consumers can only order or rank their preferences (e.g., "I prefer Bundle A to Bundle B").

## 1.2 The Cardinal Utility Theory
### 1.2.1 Core Assumptions of the Cardinal School
To make utility mathematically tractable, the Cardinal School (historically associated with Alfred Marshall) operates under five strict assumptions:
1. **Rationality of the Consumer**: The consumer is a rational agent whose primary objective is to maximize total satisfaction (utility) given a limited budget or income.
2. **Cardinal Measurability**: Utility can be quantified in cardinal numbers ($1, 2, 3, \dots$ utils). The most convenient objective proxy for utility is money (the amount a consumer is willing to pay for an additional unit of a good).
3. **Constant Marginal Utility of Money ($MU_m$)**: The utility derived from each successive unit of money income remains constant. Money is the stable ruler used to measure utility.
4. **Diminishing Marginal Utility (LDMU)**: The additional utility gained from consuming successive units of a commodity continuously decreases as consumption increases.
5. **Additivity of Utility**: The total utility of a basket of goods is the direct sum of the utilities of the individual goods in the basket:
   $$TU = f(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n) = U_1(X_1) + U_2(X_2) + \dots + U_n(X_n)$$

### 1.2.2 Total Utility (TU) vs. Marginal Utility (MU)
Understanding the mathematical and graphical relationship between $TU$ and $MU$ is the most critical hurdle in Unit 1.

* **Total Utility ($TU$)**: The aggregate sum of satisfaction obtained from consuming a specific total quantity of a good or service over a given period.
* **Marginal Utility ($MU$)**: The additional utility gained from consuming one more unit of a commodity. It represents the change in total utility resulting from a one-unit change in consumption.

$$\text{Marginal Utility (MU)} = \frac{\Delta TU}{\Delta Q} = \frac{TU_n - TU_{n-1}}{Q_n - Q_{n-1}}$$

#### The Golden Rules of TU and MU Relationships:
1. When $MU$ is **positive and rising**, $TU$ increases at an increasing rate.
2. When $MU$ is **positive but falling** (diminishing), $TU$ continues to increase but at a decreasing rate.
3. When $MU = 0$ (reaches the horizontal axis), $TU$ is at its **maximum (saturation/satiety point)**.
4. When $MU$ becomes **negative**, $TU$ begins to **decrease**, indicating that further consumption causes dissatisfaction or disutility.

### 1.2.3 The Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility (LDMU)
* **Statement of the Law**: As a consumer consumes more and more units of a specific commodity over a given period, the utility derived from each successive unit decreases, provided the consumption of all other goods remains constant (*ceteris paribus*).
* **Intuitive Example**: Imagine a student coming home from school in Addis Ababa on a hot afternoon. The first glass of water gives immense pleasure (high $MU$). The second glass is satisfying but less magical (lower $MU$). By the fifth glass, the student is full ($MU = 0$). A sixth glass would cause stomach discomfort ($MU < 0$, disutility).

## 1.3 The Consumer Maximization Problem
How does a rational consumer spend their scarce income to achieve the absolute highest possible satisfaction? This is the core optimization problem.

### 1.3.1 Case 1: Consumer Equilibrium for a Single Commodity
If a consumer is choosing how many units of a single good $X$ to buy, they weigh the subjective marginal utility of the good (expressed in monetary terms) against the objective market price $P_x$.
* **Equilibrium Condition**:
  $$MU_x = P_x \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{MU_x}{P_x} = 1$$
* **Dynamic Adjustments**:
  * If $MU_x > P_x$: The consumer is getting more satisfaction per birr than the market price. A rational consumer will **increase** consumption of $X$. As they do, $MU_x$ will fall (due to LDMU) until it equals $P_x$.
  * If $MU_x < P_x$: The consumer is paying more than the satisfaction they receive. They will **reduce** consumption of $X$. As consumption drops, $MU_x$ rises until it equals $P_x$.

#### Textbook Single-Commodity Case Study: Bread
Let us examine the utility schedule for a single commodity (Bread) priced at **Birr 2 per unit** (Table 1.4):

| Quantity of Bread | Total Utility ($TU$) | Marginal Utility ($MU$) | Marginal Utility per Birr ($\frac{MU}{P}$ where $P = 2$) | Marginal Utility of Money ($MU_m$) | Decision / State |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :--- |
| 0 | 0 | - | - | 1 | - |
| 1 | 4 | 4 | $4/2 = 2$ | 1 | Consume More ($MU/P > MU_m$) |
| 2 | 6 | 2 | $2/2 = 1$ | 1 | **Equilibrium** ($\frac{MU}{P} = MU_m$) |
| 3 | 7 | 1 | $1/2 = 0.5$ | 1 | Consume Less ($MU/P < MU_m$) |
| 4 | 7 | 0 | $0/2 = 0$ | 1 | Discontinue |
| 5 | 6 | -1 | $-1/2 = -0.5$ | 1 | Discontinue |

* **Analysis**: The consumer reaches equilibrium at **exactly 2 units of bread**. At this point, the marginal utility of the second bread (2 utils) matches its price (Birr 2), meaning the last Birr spent on bread yields exactly 1 util of satisfaction, matching the constant marginal utility of money.

### 1.3.2 Case of Two or More Commodities (Equi-Marginal Principle)
When a consumer spends their fixed income ($I$) on multiple goods (e.g., Good $X$ and Good $Y$), they maximize utility when the last Birr spent on each good yields the exact same marginal utility.
* **Equilibrium Condition (Mathematical)**:
  $$\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y} = MU_{\text{money}}$$
* **Budget Constraint**:
  $$P_X \cdot X + P_Y \cdot Y = I$$

#### Comprehensive Calculation Practice: Bread ($X$) and Injera ($Y$)
Let:
* Consumer Income ($I$) = **Birr 22**
* Price of Bread ($P_X$) = **Birr 2** per unit
* Price of Injera ($Y$) = **Birr 4** per unit

Below is the utility schedule:

| Quantity | $TU_X$ | $MU_X$ | $\frac{MU_X}{P_X}$ | $TU_Y$ | $MU_Y$ | $\frac{MU_Y}{P_Y}$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **0** | 0 | - | - | 0 | - | - |
| **1** | 6 | 6 | **3** | 12 | 12 | **3** |
    | **2** | 10 | 4 | **2** | 19 | 7 | 1.75 |
    | **3** | 12 | 2 | **1** | 24 | 5 | 1.25 |
    | **4** | 12 | 0 | 0 | 28 | 4 | **1** |
    | **5** | 11 | -1 | -0.5 | 28 | 0 | 0 |
    | **6** | 9 | -3 | -1.5 | 26 | -2 | -0.5 |

* **Step 1: Identify matching values of $\frac{MU}{P}$**:
  1. $\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y} = 3$ (Occurs at $X = 1$ and $Y = 1$)
  2. $\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y} = 1$ (Occurs at $X = 3$ and $Y = 4$)
* **Step 2: Test combinations against the Budget Constraint ($2X + 4Y = 22$)**:
  * *Combination 1* ($1X, 1Y$):
    $$2(1) + 4(1) = 2 + 4 = 6 \text{ Birr} \neq 22 \text{ Birr} \quad (\text{Under-spending})$$
  * *Combination 2* ($3X, 4Y$):
    $$2(3) + 4(4) = 6 + 16 = 22 \text{ Birr} = 22 \text{ Birr} \quad (\text{Perfect match!})$$
* **Step 3: Calculate Maximum Total Utility**:
  $$TU_{\text{max}} = TU_X(3) + TU_Y(4) = 12 \text{ utils} + 28 \text{ utils} = 40 \text{ utils}$$

## 1.4 Introduction to Ordinal Utility Theory
The Ordinal Utility theory (pioneered by J.R. Hicks and R.G.D. Allen) arose as a critique of the Cardinal School, arguing that measuring utility in exact numbers is psychologically unrealistic.

### 1.4.1 Assumptions of the Ordinal School
1. **Rationality**: The consumer is rational and seeks to reach the highest possible ranking of satisfaction.
2. **Utility is Ordinal**: Utility cannot be measured in absolute units. Goods can only be ranked in order of preference (e.g., $A \succ B \succ C$).
3. **Consistency of Choice**: The consumer's choices must be consistent. If a consumer prefers bundle $A$ over bundle $B$ in one period, they will not choose bundle $B$ over bundle $A$ in another period under identical conditions.
   $$\text{If } A > B \implies B \ngtr A$$
4. **Transitivity of Choice**: If a consumer prefers bundle $A$ over bundle $B$, and bundle $B$ over bundle $C$, they must logically prefer bundle $A$ over bundle $C$.
   $$\text{If } A > B \text{ and } B > C \implies A > C$$
5. **Diminishing Marginal Rate of Substitution (DMRS)**: The rate at which a consumer is willing to substitute one commodity ($Y$) for another ($X$) while keeping total satisfaction constant decreases as they acquire more of $X$.
6. **Non-Satiation (More is Better)**: The consumer always prefers a larger quantity of a good to a smaller quantity, assuming they have not reached complete satiety.

## Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
* **Mistake**: Thinking that the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility means *Total Utility* decreases.
  * *Correction*: LDMU states that *Marginal Utility* decreases. Total utility continues to increase as long as marginal utility is positive, just at a slower rate.
* **Mistake**: Assuming constant marginal utility of money applies to Ordinal theory.
  * *Correction*: The constant marginal utility of money is a strict assumption of the *Cardinal* school. Ordinal theory bypasses quantitative money-utility tracking altogether.
* **Mistake**: Forgetting to check the budget constraint when applying the equi-marginal principle.
  * *Correction*: Multiple commodity bundles can satisfy $\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y}$, but only the bundle that costs exactly the consumer's income represents the true constrained maximum.

# Unit 2: Theories of Demand and Supply

## 2.1 Theory of Demand
### 2.1.1 Definition of Demand
* **Standard Definition**: In economics, **Demand** is the quantity of a commodity that a consumer is both **willing** and **able** to purchase at various prices over a specific period of time, ceteris paribus.
  $$\text{Demand} = \text{Willingness to buy} + \text{Ability to pay}$$
* **Syllabus Point**: Desire or want alone does not constitute economic demand. An expensive car is a desire for a student, but not economic demand unless they have the financial capability (ability) to pay for it.

### 2.1.2 The Law of Demand
* **Law of Demand**: All other things remaining constant (*ceteris paribus*), the quantity demanded of a commodity has an **inverse relationship** with its price.
  $$\text{Price } (P) \uparrow \implies \text{Quantity Demanded } (Q_d) \downarrow$$
  $$\text{Price } (P) \downarrow \implies \text{Quantity Demanded } (Q_d) \uparrow$$
* **Graphical Slope**: Consequently, the demand curve is **downward-sloping** (negative slope).

### 2.1.3 The Demand Function, Schedule, and Curve
1. **Demand Function**: A mathematical statement showing the functional relationship between quantity demanded and price:
   $$Q_d = f(P) = a - bP$$
   * Where $a$ is the autonomous demand (intercept when $P = 0$) and $b$ is the slope coefficient ($b > 0$).
2. **Demand Schedule**: A tabular representation of the prices and corresponding quantities demanded.
3. **Demand Curve**: A graphical plot of the demand schedule, with price on the vertical ($Y$) axis and quantity on the horizontal ($X$) axis.

#### Textbook Case Study: Amina’s Coffee Demand (Table 2.1)
* Price ($P$ in Birr): 0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45.
* Quantity ($Q$ in kg): 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1, 0.
* **Analysis**: As price increases by Birr 5, quantity demanded falls by exactly 1 kg, demonstrating a perfect linear inverse relationship.

### 2.1.4 Determinants of Demand (Non-Price Shifting Factors)
While price causes a change in quantity demanded along the curve, other factors shift the entire demand curve:
1. **Price of Related Goods**:
   * **Substitutes**: Goods that can be used in place of one another (e.g., Coffee and Tea). An increase in the price of tea leads to an increase in the demand for coffee (rightward shift).
   * **Complements**: Goods consumed together (e.g., Tea and Sugar, Cars and Petrol). An increase in the price of petrol decreases the demand for cars (leftward shift).
2. **Consumer Income**:
   * **Normal Goods**: Demand increases when income rises (e.g., fresh beef, quality clothing).
   * **Inferior Goods**: Demand decreases when income rises (e.g., low-grade grains, second-hand clothing).
3. **Consumer Tastes and Habits**: Positive changes in preference shift the demand curve to the right.
4. **Population Size**: An increase in population size increases market demand (rightward shift).
5. **Seasonality**: Demand for specific goods rises during certain seasons (e.g., heavy umbrellas during rainy seasons).
6. **Future Expectations**: If consumers expect prices to rise next month, they will buy more now (rightward shift).

### 2.1.5 Change in Quantity Demanded vs. Change in Demand
* **Change in Quantity Demanded**:
  * **Cause**: Change in the good's **own price**.
  * **Graphical Representation**: Movement **along** a fixed demand curve (called *extension* when price falls and *contraction* when price rises).
* **Change in Demand**:
  * **Cause**: Change in **non-price determinants** (income, tastes, substitutes, etc.).
  * **Graphical Representation**: A complete **shift** of the demand curve (rightward for an increase, leftward for a decrease).

## 2.2 Theory of Supply
### 2.2.1 Definition of Supply
* **Definition**: The quantity of a commodity that producers (firms) are **willing** and **able** to produce and offer for sale at various prices over a specific period of time, ceteris paribus.

### 2.2.2 The Law of Supply
* **Law of Supply**: Ceteris paribus, there is a **direct (positive) relationship** between price and quantity supplied.
  $$\text{Price } (P) \uparrow \implies \text{Quantity Supplied } (Q_s) \uparrow$$
  $$\text{Price } (P) \downarrow \implies \text{Quantity Supplied } (Q_s) \downarrow$$
* **Graphical Slope**: The supply curve is **upward-sloping** (positive slope).

### 2.2.3 Supply Function, Schedule, and Curve
1. **Supply Function**:
   $$Q_s = f(P) = a + bP$$
   * Where $a$ is the intercept and $b$ is the positive slope coefficient.
2. **Supply Schedule**: Tabular representation of prices and quantities supplied.
3. **Supply Curve**: Graphical plot of the supply schedule.

### 2.2.4 Determinants of Supply (Non-Price Shifting Factors)
Factors that shift the entire supply curve:
1. **Cost of Inputs (Factors of Production)**: Higher input costs (e.g., expensive fertilizer) raise production costs and decrease supply (leftward shift).
2. **State of Technology**: Technological progress increases productivity, lowers costs, and increases supply (rightward shift).
3. **Government Policies (Taxes and Subsidies)**:
   * **Taxes**: Increase costs, decreasing supply (leftward shift).
   * **Subsidies**: Lower costs, increasing supply (rightward shift).
4. **Number of Sellers**: An increase in the number of producers increases market supply (rightward shift).
5. **Future Price Expectations**: If sellers expect prices to rise in the future, they may hoard goods now, reducing current supply (leftward shift).
6. **Natural / External Factors**: Good weather increases agricultural supply; droughts or floods destroy crops and decrease supply.

## 2.3 Market Equilibrium
Market equilibrium occurs when the conflicting interests of buyers (who want lower prices) and sellers (who want higher prices) are perfectly balanced.

### 2.3.1 Definition of Equilibrium
* **Equilibrium Condition**:
  $$Q_d = Q_s$$
* **Equilibrium Price ($P^*$)**: The market-clearing price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
* **Equilibrium Quantity ($Q^*$)**: The quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.

### 2.3.2 Market Disequilibrium: Surpluses and Shortages
* **Market Surplus (Excess Supply)**: Occurs when the market price is **above** the equilibrium price ($P > P^*$).
  * $Q_s > Q_d$.
  * Sellers have unsold inventory and will bid prices **down** toward equilibrium.
* **Market Shortage (Excess Demand)**: Occurs when the market price is **below** the equilibrium price ($P < P^*$).
  * $Q_d > Q_s$.
  * Buyers are unable to buy all they want and will bid prices **up** toward equilibrium.

#### Quantitative Case Study: Wheat Market Equilibrium
Let us solve the linear equilibrium problem from the textbook (Page 31):
* Demand Function: $Q_d = 80 - 3P$
* Supply Function: $Q_s = 9P - 40$

1. **Find Equilibrium Price ($P^*$)**:
   $$Q_d = Q_s \implies 80 - 3P = 9P - 40$$
   $$120 = 12P \implies P^* = 10 \text{ Birr/kg}$$
2. **Find Equilibrium Quantity ($Q^*$)**:
   Substitute $P^* = 10$ into $Q_d$ or $Q_s$:
   $$Q^* = 80 - 3(10) = 80 - 30 = 50 \text{ kg}$$
   $$Q^* = 9(10) - 40 = 90 - 40 = 50 \text{ kg}$$
   * **Conclusion**: The market clears at **Birr 10/kg** with a quantity of **50 kg**.

## 2.4 Elasticities of Demand and Supply
Elasticity measures the quantitative responsiveness of one variable to changes in another.

### 2.4.1 Price Elasticity of Demand ($E_p^D$)
* **Definition**: The degree of responsiveness of consumers (quantity demanded) to changes in the price of the commodity itself.
  $$E_p^D = \frac{\% \Delta Q_d}{\% \Delta P}$$

#### Point Elasticity vs. Arc Elasticity
1. **Point Elasticity**: Used for infinitesimal, extremely small changes in price at a single point on the curve:
   $$E_p^D = \frac{dQ}{dP} \cdot \frac{P}{Q} \approx \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \cdot \frac{P_1}{Q_1}$$
2. **Arc Elasticity (Midpoint Method)**: Measures elasticity over a range or interval on the demand curve:
   $$E_p^D = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \cdot \frac{P_1 + P_2}{Q_1 + Q_2} = \frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{P_2 - P_1} \cdot \frac{P_1 + P_2}{Q_1 + Q_2}$$

* **Note**: Because the demand curve is downward-sloping, $E_p^D$ is always negative. Economists ignore the negative sign and use the **absolute value** $|E_p^D|$ to interpret the degree of elasticity.

#### Interpretation of $|E_p^D|$:
* **$|E_p^D| > 1$ (Elastic Demand)**: Consumers are highly responsive to price. A small percentage change in price leads to a larger percentage change in quantity demanded.
* **$|E_p^D| < 1$ (Inelastic Demand)**: Consumers are unresponsive. A large price change has a small effect on quantity.
* **$|E_p^D| = 1$ (Unitary Elastic)**: Percentage change in price equals percentage change in quantity demanded.
* **$E_p^D = 0$ (Perfectly Inelastic)**: Quantity demanded remains completely unchanged regardless of price (vertical line). *Example: Life-saving medicine.*
* **$E_p^D = \infty$ (Perfectly Elastic)**: Consumers buy infinite amounts at a specific price, but zero at any other price (horizontal line).

### 2.4.2 Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand ($E_{XY}$)
Measures responsiveness of the demand for Good $X$ to price changes of another Good $Y$.
$$E_{XY} = \frac{\% \Delta Q_X}{\% \Delta P_Y} = \frac{\Delta Q_X}{\Delta P_Y} \cdot \frac{P_Y}{Q_X}$$
* **Interpretation of Sign**:
  * **$E_{XY} > 0$ (Positive)**: Substitutes (e.g., Pepsi and Coca-Cola).
  * **$E_{XY} < 0$ (Negative)**: Complements (e.g., fuel and cars).
  * **$E_{XY} = 0$ (Zero)**: Independent, unrelated goods.

### 2.4.3 Income Elasticity of Demand ($E_I$)
Measures responsiveness of demand to changes in consumer income.
$$E_I = \frac{\% \Delta Q_d}{\% \Delta I}$$
* **Interpretation of Sign and Value**:
  * **$E_I < 0$ (Negative)**: Inferior Goods.
  * **$E_I > 0$ (Positive)**: Normal Goods.
    * **$0 < E_I \le 1$**: Necessity Good.
    * **$E_I > 1$**: Luxury Good.

### 2.4.4 Price Elasticity of Supply ($E_p^S$)
Measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to changes in price.
$$E_p^S = \frac{\% \Delta Q_s}{\% \Delta P}$$
* Since price and supply are directly related, $E_p^S$ is always positive.
* Classified identically to demand: Elastic ($>1$), Inelastic ($<1$), Unitary ($1$), Perfectly Inelastic ($0$), Perfectly Elastic ($\infty$).

For completeness, the remaining units of the Grade 10 Economics curriculum are structured below into learning pathways, core concepts, and key equations.

## Unit 3: Theories of Production and Cost
* **Core Focus**: Explores the behavior of firms, how inputs are transformed into outputs, and the cost mappings of production.
* **Key Concepts**:
  * **Short Run**: Period where at least one input (usually Capital, $K$) is fixed, and only Labor ($L$) is variable.
  * **Long Run**: Period where all inputs are variable.
  * **Total Product ($TP$)**, **Average Product ($AP_L = \frac{TP}{L}$)**, and **Marginal Product ($MP_L = \frac{\Delta TP}{\Delta L}$)**.
  * **Law of Diminishing Marginal Productivity (Law of Variable Proportions)**: Adding more of a variable input to a fixed input eventually causes the marginal product to decline.
  * **Stages of Production**: Stage I (Increasing returns), Stage II (Diminishing returns - *rational zone of operation*), Stage III (Negative returns).
  * **Isoquants**: Curves showing all input combinations that yield the same output level.
  * **Returns to Scale**: Increasing, Constant, and Decreasing Returns to Scale in the long run.
  * **Cost Functions**:
    * Total Cost: $TC = TFC + TVC$.
    * Average Costs: $ATC = AFC + AVC$ where $AFC = \frac{TFC}{Q}$ and $AVC = \frac{TVC}{Q}$.
    * Marginal Cost: $MC = \frac{\Delta TC}{\Delta Q}$.
  * **Cost-Product Mirror Relationship**: $MC$ is the mirror image of $MP_L$, and $AVC$ is the mirror image of $AP_L$. When productivity rises, costs fall.

## Unit 4: Market Structure
* **Core Focus**: Analyzes the competitive environments in which firms operate.
* **Four Main Market Models**:
  1. **Perfect Competition**: Infinite buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, price takers, free entry/exit, perfect information. $P = AR = MR$.
  2. **Monopoly**: Single seller, unique product, price maker, extreme entry barriers. $P = AR > MR$.
  3. **Monopolistic Competition**: Many sellers, differentiated products, non-price competition (advertising), free entry/exit.
  4. **Oligopoly**: Few dominant firms, mutual interdependence, high entry barriers. *Duopoly* is a special case with exactly two firms (e.g., Ethio Telecom and Safaricom Ethiopia).

## Unit 5: Banking and Finance
* **Core Focus**: Investigates financial systems, intermediaries, and banking history in Ethiopia.
* **Key Concepts**:
  * **Financial Intermediary**: Acts as a middleman between savers (surplus units) and borrowers (deficit units), e.g., Commercial Bank of Ethiopia (CBE).
  * **Financial Markets**: Money Market (maturity $\le 1$ year, e.g., Treasury bills, commercial papers) vs. Capital Market (maturity $> 1$ year, e.g., bonds, equities).
  * **History of Banking in Ethiopia**:
    * **1905**: Modern banking starts with the Bank of Abyssinia (concession with National Bank of Egypt).
    * **1931**: Bank of Abyssinia liquidated; replaced by Bank of Ethiopia (first government-controlled bank).
    * **1943**: State Bank of Ethiopia established.
    * **1963**: State Bank split into National Bank of Ethiopia (NBE - Central Bank) and Commercial Bank of Ethiopia (CBE).
  * **Micro-finance Institutions (MFIs)**: Micro-loans for low-income citizens using group-based guarantees (bypassing collateral).
  * **Indigenous Institutions**: **Iqub** (rotating savings association) and **Idir** (communal insurance/burial society).

## Unit 6: Economic Growth
* **Core Focus**: Evaluates macroeconomic variables and indicators of long-term economic performance.
* **Key Concepts**:
  * **Macroeconomic Variables**: Current Account Balance (Exports - Imports), Inflation, Economic Growth, Unemployment.
  * **Inflation**: Cost-push vs. Demand-pull.
  * **Economic Growth vs. Economic Development**: Growth is quantitative (increase in real GDP); Development is qualitative (broad improvement in living standards, infrastructure, and institutional quality).
  * **Short-run Growth Formula**:
    $$g = \frac{RGDP_t - RGDP_{t-1}}{RGDP_{t-1}} \times 100\%$$
  * **Weaknesses of GDP**: Omits leisure, home production (cooking at home), environmental quality, income distribution, and the underground/black market economy.
  * **Business Cycle Phases**: Peak/Boom, Recession, Trough/Depression, Recovery/Expansion.

## Unit 7: The Ethiopian Economy
* **Core Focus**: An overview of the economic history and sectoral performance of Ethiopia.
* **Key Concepts**:
  * **GDP Equation**: $Y = C + I + G + NX$.
  * **Nominal vs. Real GDP**: Nominal uses current prices; Real uses constant base-year prices to remove inflation.
  * **GDP Deflator**: $\frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100$.
  * **Sectors of the Ethiopian Economy**:
    * **Agriculture**: Historically the backbone; employs $\approx 85\%$ of the population, generates $>80\%$ of exports, high vulnerability to drought (rain-fed). Systems: Smallholder (dominant, mixed farming), Pastoral, and Commercial.
    * **Industry**: Small base; dominated by manufacturing, construction, electricity/water.
    * **Services**: Largest share of GDP recently ($\approx 45-46\%$); includes tourism, transport (Ethiopian Airlines), retail, and telecom.
  * **Development Strategy**: **ADLI** (Agricultural Development Led Industrialization) — uses agriculture as the primary engine to drive industrial growth.

## Unit 8: Business Startups and Innovation
* **Core Focus**: Introduction to entrepreneurship, innovation types, and business feasibility.
* **Key Concepts**:
  * **Innovation (Oslo Manual)**: New or improved product/process that differs significantly from previous versions. Types: Product, Process, Organizational, Marketing, and Eco-Innovation.
  * **Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)**: The process of industrial mutation where new innovations continuously revolutionize the economic structure from within, destroying the old ones (e.g., smartphones replacing film cameras).
  * **Forms of Business Organization**: Sole Proprietorship (single owner, unlimited liability), Partnership (2-20 persons, shared risk), Corporation (separate legal entity, limited liability).
  * **Feasibility Study**: An inexpensive study answering if a business idea will work, be profitable, and cost-justify a full business plan.
  * **SWOT Analysis**: Internal (Strengths, Weaknesses) and External (Opportunities, Threats).
  * **Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)**: Summing monetary values of benefits vs. costs.

# AI Tutor Guidelines & Pedagogical Playbook

When acting as an interactive AI Tutor for Grade 10 Economics, strictly adhere to the following educational rules:

1. **Step-by-Step Scaffolding**: Do not dump the final mathematical answer immediately. Break down calculations (e.g., utility maximization or elasticity) into clear, sequential steps:
   * *Step 1: Write down given values.*
   * *Step 2: State the formula.*
   * *Step 3: Perform substitutions.*
   * *Step 4: Solve and interpret.*
2. **Socratic Hinting**: If a student is struggling with a concept, give a helpful hint before revealing the full solution.
   * *Example*: *"Think about what happens to your enjoyment as you eat more of your favorite snack. Does each piece taste as amazing as the very first one? How does this relate to Marginal Utility?"*
3. **Contextual Localization**: Use examples familiar to Ethiopian students (such as Birr, Teff, Injera, Kolo, Addis Ababa, Awash Valley, and local businesses) to keep learning highly relatable.
4. **Failsafe Factuality**: Never invent statistics or historical dates not supported by the official curriculum (e.g., banking history details in Unit 5). If a student asks a highly specific historical fact not present in the textbook, explicitly state that it is outside the textbook's scope but provide standard academic consensus.
